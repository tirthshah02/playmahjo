If you have played Mahjong in a Chinese household and then sat down at an American Mahjong club — or vice versa — you may have felt like you were playing two completely different games. You kind of were.

Hong Kong Mahjong and American Mahjong share the same tiles and the same basic objective (complete a winning hand before your opponents do), but the rules, strategy, tile sets, and culture around them diverge significantly. This guide covers every major difference between the two.

## At a Glance

| Feature | Hong Kong Mahjong | American Mahjong |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| Tile count | 144 (136 + 8 flowers/seasons) | ~166 (standard set + 8 jokers) |
| Jokers | None | 8 jokers, central mechanic |
| Winning hands | Any legal hand | Must match the annual NMJL card |
| Card/rules change | Fixed rules | New card published every year |
| Scoring | Points-based, variable | Fixed values from the card |
| Chow | Legal (with restrictions) | Not used |
| Pace | Fast, loud, fluid | Slower, more methodical |
| Where it's popular | East/Southeast Asia, globally | United States |

## The Tile Sets

**Hong Kong Mahjong** uses a standard 144-tile set:
- 108 suit tiles (Bamboo, Characters, Circles — nine tiles each, four copies each)
- 16 wind tiles (East, South, West, North — four of each)
- 12 dragon tiles (Red, Green, White — four of each)
- 8 bonus tiles (four Flowers, four Seasons)

**American Mahjong** uses approximately 166 tiles:
- The same 144-tile base
- 8 joker tiles (wildcards)
- Some sets also include extra tiles for specific hand patterns

The jokers are not an add-on in American Mahjong — they are a fundamental part of the game. Much of American Mahjong strategy revolves around acquiring jokers and denying them to opponents.

## How You Win

This is the most significant difference between the two variants.

### Hong Kong Mahjong: Any Legal Hand Wins

In Hong Kong Mahjong, any valid hand wins. The standard structure is four sets (pungs or chows) and one pair. Creativity within the rules is part of the game — there are dozens of special high-scoring hand patterns, but you do not have to chase them. A simple hand wins; a complex hand wins more.

### American Mahjong: The Annual Card

In American Mahjong, winning hands are dictated entirely by the **National Mah Jongg League (NMJL) card**, published each spring. The card lists every legal winning hand for that year, organised by point value. Your hand must match one of those combinations exactly — tile for tile, including suit. If your hand is not on the card, it does not win, no matter how strong it looks by any other logic.

The card changes every year. Hands that were winners last year may not be on this year's card. This creates an annual reset in strategy and means American Mahjong players need to buy and study a new card each spring.

## Jokers

**Hong Kong Mahjong** has no joker tiles. Period.

**American Mahjong** revolves around them. Jokers can substitute for any tile in a pung, kong, or quint (a group of five identical tiles, which appears in American Mahjong but not in Hong Kong style). The mechanics include:

- **Claiming jokers from melds:** If a player has melded a set containing a joker, any other player can swap in the real tile the joker represents and take the joker for themselves. This is called "joker exchange" and is a core competitive element.
- **Jokers cannot be used in pairs** — they only work within sets of three or more.
- **Joker-heavy hands** score the same as all-real-tile hands on the card — there is no bonus for purity.

In Hong Kong Mahjong, purity of hand (concealed hand, no melded sets) is explicitly rewarded with higher scores. In American Mahjong, this concept does not exist.

## Chows (Sequences)

**Hong Kong Mahjong:** Chows (three consecutive tiles in the same suit, e.g., 4-5-6 Characters) are a legal and common set type. Claiming a chow from a discard is restricted to the next player in turn order.

**American Mahjong:** Chows do not exist. Hands on the NMJL card are composed of pungs, kongs, quints, pairs, and specific combinations — but never sequences of consecutive numbers. This changes the entire strategic texture of the game.

## Scoring

**Hong Kong Mahjong** uses a point-based system where the value of your hand depends on what it contains. Rare combinations score exponentially more than common ones. All-pung hands, pure suit hands, and hands with dragon or wind pungs all earn bonus points. The total is calculated and the losers pay the winner — at double if you win by self-draw.

**American Mahjong** uses flat scoring. Each hand on the NMJL card is assigned a fixed value (e.g., 25 cents, 50 cents, $1, depending on the group's stakes). Every non-winner pays the winner that amount, regardless of how close to winning they were or how their own hand was structured. Scoring does not vary based on hand composition the way it does in Hong Kong Mahjong.

## Feel and Pace

**Hong Kong Mahjong** is characteristically fast and loud. Tiles clack, pungs are called quickly, hands complete in minutes. The scoring system rewards boldness — chasing a high-value hand instead of a quick safe win is a real and often correct strategy. The social culture around it is energetic.

**American Mahjong** is slower and more deliberate. Players are working toward a specific, known target from the card. Each decision is more about matching a pattern than building flexibly. The culture around American Mahjong — particularly in the communities where it's most common — tends to be social and leisurely.

## Cultural Context

Hong Kong Mahjong descends from the standardised Chinese Mahjong rules that spread globally through the Chinese diaspora in the 20th century. It is the closest thing to a universal standard, and understanding it makes learning any other variant easier.

American Mahjong developed its distinct identity through the National Mah Jongg League, founded in 1937, which codified and popularised the game for American social settings. It became especially popular in American Jewish communities and has maintained a large, dedicated player base ever since.

## Which Should You Learn?

**If your goal is broad transferability** — learn Hong Kong Mahjong first. It is the standard that most other variants (Riichi, Taiwanese, Singaporean) build on. Once you understand Hong Kong Mahjong, picking up other styles is significantly easier.

**If your community plays American Mahjong** — learn American Mahjong. Community fit matters more than abstract transferability. You can always add Hong Kong Mahjong later.
