The most underrated skill in Mahjong is not building a beautiful hand — it is reading what everyone else is building. Every tile your opponents discard is a piece of information. Experienced players treat the three discard pools as an open book that tells them what hands are in progress, which tiles are dangerous, and when to switch from offence to defence.

This guide teaches you how to start reading that book.

## Why Discards Are Information

When a player discards a tile, they are implicitly saying: *this tile does not fit my hand.* Over the course of a game, each player's discard pile becomes a partial inventory of tiles they have rejected. From those rejections, you can infer what they are holding.

This works because Mahjong hands follow predictable structures. If a player hasn't discarded any Bamboo tiles by their fifth discard, they are likely holding Bamboo. If someone discards three middle tiles (4, 5, 6) in their first four turns, they are not building sequences in any suit — they may be going for an all-pung hand or an honours-heavy hand.

## Reading the First Four Discards

The opening discards are the most informative. Players are sorting their hands and making their first major decisions.

**Honour tiles early** — A player who discards wind and dragon tiles in their first two turns is clearing tiles they cannot incorporate. They are likely going for a suit-heavy, efficient hand. These players tend to build fast and win quietly.

**Middle tiles early (4, 5, 6)** — Unusual. Middle tiles are the most flexible tiles in the game and form the centre of most sequences. Discarding them early signals the player may be going for an all-pung or honours-based hand, or that they drew a very specific partial hand where these tiles genuinely don't fit.

**Terminals early (1s and 9s)** — Normal. Terminals are difficult to incorporate into sequences and are the most commonly discarded tiles in any game. This tells you little on its own.

## Tracking Suit Density

Watch which suits are appearing in the discard pools and which are not.

**Dense in discards = safe to throw.** If two or three players are throwing away Circles tiles throughout the early game, there are few Circles left in hands. Circles are relatively safe to discard.

**Absent from discards = dangerous.** If no one is throwing away Bamboo for the first eight turns of the game, there is a good chance at least one player is hoarding Bamboo tiles for a deep hand in that suit. Be careful discarding Bamboo.

A simple tracking habit: every few turns, scan all three discard pools and note which of the three suits is least represented. That suit is live — players are holding it.

## Middle Tiles vs Terminals

The danger profile of tiles shifts as the game progresses.

**Early game:** Terminals are safe; middle tiles are risky.  
**Late game:** This flips. By the time 30–40 tiles have been drawn from the wall, players near winning are often waiting on specific tiles in sequences. A 5 or 6 of the live suit can be exactly what they need.

This is why experienced players save their terminal discards for later and use middle tiles while it is still early — the reverse of what most beginners do.

## Honour Tiles: Safe Until They Are Not

Honour tiles (winds and dragons) that have not appeared in any discard pile are in one of two places: in players' hands, or not yet drawn. If all four copies of a particular dragon have not shown up and the game is halfway through, someone likely has a dragon pung built or in progress. Discarding that dragon is risky.

However, if you see two or three copies of an honour tile across the discard pools, the remaining copies are almost certainly not in a winning position — there are too few left for anyone to hold a pung.

## Reading Claim Behaviour

**What a player claims tells you what they are building.** If a player claims a pung of 7-Bamboo, they are now committed to the Bamboo suit and likely building sequences or more pungs in it. This narrows your understanding of their hand significantly.

**What a player does not claim is equally informative.** If you discard a 6-Characters that would complete an obvious chow for the next player and they pass, either they do not need it or their hand is structured differently than you assumed. Recalibrate.

**Claiming speed:** A player who claims instantly has been waiting for that tile. A player who hesitates before claiming is deciding whether the claim is worth revealing their hand direction.

## When Someone Goes Quiet

Early in a game, experienced players discard at a steady rhythm. When a player starts discarding slowly — pausing before each tile, picking up and putting down — they are close. This is tenpai (ready to win): they need only one more tile.

At this point, shift into defensive mode:
1. Stop making aggressive discards in the suit they are likely holding
2. Prioritise safe tiles over building your own hand
3. Assess whether your hand is advanced enough that winning still outweighs the risk

## How to Identify Safe Tiles

When you need to discard safely, use this hierarchy:

**Most safe:**
- Tiles the target player has already discarded (they cannot win on these)
- Tiles where all four copies are visible across discard pools (the tile is "dead")

**Reasonably safe:**
- Honour tiles that three or four players have discarded
- Tiles at the extreme ends of a suit (1s and 9s) when the player appears to be building sequences — they are hard to incorporate at the edges

**Risky:**
- Middle tiles in a suit the target player has been hoarding
- Tiles adjacent to tiles the target player has recently claimed
- Tiles that complete obvious two-tile waits in the live suit

## The Skill Progression

**Beginners** watch only their own hand.

**Intermediate players** track one opponent — usually the player to their right, since they are the one whose discards most directly affect you.

**Strong players** track all three opponents simultaneously and make every discard decision by weighing their own hand progress against the risk of feeding each player.

You do not get to the third level without going through the second. Start with one player. Before each discard, ask: *what is the player to my right building?* Over dozens of games, the habit will extend naturally to all three.

The single most effective thing a beginner can do to improve: before every discard, look at all three discard pools. Just look. You do not need to know what you are looking for yet — pattern recognition builds automatically once you start paying attention.
